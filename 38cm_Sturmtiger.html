38 cm Sturmtiger (Sturmmörserwagen 606/4 mit 38 cm RW 61)

Table of Contents: What was it's purpose and who built it? | Tank Stats | Fun Facts | Cited Sources

What was it's purpose and who built it:

It's primary task was to provide heavy fire support for infantry units fighting in urban areas. The Wehrmacht only had tanks like the Sturm-Infanteriegeschütz 33B (I'm not going to try to even pretend I can pronounce that) or the Sturmgeschütz III (I will also not try to even pretend I can pronounce this one either), which were fitted with a 15 cm sIG 33/1 and a 7.5 cm StuK 40 L/48 respectively. Safe to say, these weren't that good at destroying buildings, so the German's wanted something with a bit more firepower. While better models were made later like the Sturmpanzer IV (This I can actually pronounce), known to the Allies as the Brummbär, by this time infantry anti-tank weapons were improving dramatically, and the Wehrmacht still saw a need for a similar, but more heavily armoured and armed vehicle. Therefore, a decision was made to create a new vehicle based on the Tiger tank and arm it with a 210 mm howitzer. However, this weapon turned out not to be available at the time and was therefore replaced by a 380 mm rocket launcher, which was adapted from a Kriegsmarine depth charge launcher. In September 1943 plans were made for Krupp to fabricate new Tiger I armoured hulls for the Sturmtiger. The Tiger I hulls were to be sent to Henschel for chassis assembly and then to Alkett where the superstructures would be mounted. The first prototype was ready and presented to Adolf Hitler in October 1943. Delivery of the first hulls would occur in December 1943, with the first three Sturmtiger completed by Alkett by 20 February 1944

Tank Stats:

The Sturmtiger is classified as a "Heavy Assault Gun", made by Nazi Germany. It was in service from August 1944-1945 in WWII. The designer is "Alkett", who was a major manufacturer of armored vehicles for the Wehrmarcht during WWII. The designing process took one year, lasting from 1943 to 1944. Production of the Sturmtiger began on October 1943 and ended on January 1945. Only 18 Sturmtigers were ever built, with all of them using rebult Tiger I chassis.

The Sturmtiger weighs 68 tonnes (75 short tons (Used by the United States); 67 long tons (Used by the British before decimalization and mertrification, and is now only in specific maritime and historical contexts)), having a length of 6.28 m (20 ft 7 in), a width of 3.57 m (11 ft 9 in), and a heigh of 2.85 m (9 ft 4 in). The Sturmtiger is a 5 manned crew, consisting of a driver, radio operator, loader, a 2nd loader (Those rounds were heavy man), and a commander who acts as the gunner. It's armor was at max, 150 mm at a 47 degree vertical angle, with a minimum armor thickness of 62 mm. It's main armament is a 380 mm RW 61 rocket launcher L/5.4, carrying 14 rounds. It has a secondary armament consisting of a singular 7.92 mm MG 34.

It has a water-cooled Mayback HL230 P45 V-12, and runs on gasoline, cranking out 700 PS (690 hp, 515 kW). It's power to weight is 10.77 PS/tonne, which for a 68 ton tank, it just terrible and made the vehicle significantly underpowered in the horsepower department. This gave the Sturmtiger sluggish acceleration, poor off-road performance, and extreme mechanical strain. The Sturmtiger utilizes a "torsion-bar" suspension, which while it gave the best ride quality and stability, it took up more space in the tank and is a pain to repair, with the crew having to rip off the whole floor of the tank to even access it. It has a fuel capacity of 540 L (120 imp gal; 140 US gal) and has an operational range of 120 km (75 mi). It's maximum spead is 40 km/h (25 mph), so it's faster than America's T28 Super Heavy Tank, but that's not too high of a bar to pass, considering that the T28 is 86 tons (18 tons more than the Sturmtiger), has half the Power/Weight ratio of the Sturmtiger of 5.8 hp/tonne, and only has a maximum speed of a measly 8 mph (13 km/h)

Fun Facts:

The name you see at the title --- 38 cm Sturmtiger --- is a more informal name the Allies gave it. Its full name in German is "Sturmmörserwagen 606/4 mit 38 cm RW 61". "Sturmmörserwagen" literally means "Assault Mortar Vehicle", with "606/4" being a specific index number assigned by the Heereswaffenamt (Army Weapons Agency). The "606" series generally referred to vehicles based on the Tiger I chassis. "mit 38 cm" refers to the caliber of the main weapon, and "RW 61" stands for "Raketen-Werfer 61", which is a type of rocket the German's used.

It also has a little loading crane to help load it's ammunition (shown above in the image to the right), as the rounds it shot out were bulky and weighed around 345 kg to 351 kg (760 - 770 lbs). The rocket projectiles themselves were roughly 1.5 m (4 ft 11 in) in length, over half the heigh of the average human. It has a max range of up to 6000 m (20000 ft), penetrating up to 2.5 m (8 ft 2 in) of reinforced concrete. Because of the rounds bulkiness, only 14 rounds could be carried internally, including one that's already loaded and another in the loading tray

The Sturmtiger itself was fitted on the chassis of rebuilt Tiger I's, so some had a Zimmerit coating, which was something the German's invented in fear that the Allies would invent anti-tank mines to attack their tanks. It was a paste-like coating used on mid and late war German armored vehicles during WWII. It was used to produce a hard layer covering the metal armor of the vehicle, providing enough separation that magnetically attached anti-tank mines would fail to stick to the vehicle, despite Germany being the only country to use magnetic anti-tank mines in numbers, meaning they really just developed it for nothing. This was developed by the German company Chemische Werke Zimmer & Co. The application of Zimmerit was discontinued from factory applications on 9 September 1944 and from field application on 7 October 1944 due to concerns that projectile impacts could ignite it. These proved false, but the order was never rescinded, since at this time, Germany was flat out broke on money, materials, and manpower, and considering how the Allies never fought against the Germans with anti-tank mines, they simply decided to forgo the extra labor in adding that Zimmerit coating. After WWII ended, the British and Canada carried out trials of similar material on the Churchill and Cromwell, but never implemented due to the widespread use of man-portable HEAT rockets such as the bazooka, which made magnetic mines obsolete, which then in turn made Zimmerit and it's other varients developed by other nations obsolete
